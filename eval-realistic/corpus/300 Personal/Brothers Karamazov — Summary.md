# Brothers Karamazov — Summary

[[book notes]] on Dostoevsky's *The Brothers Karamazov*, through Book 2. The cast is in
[[Brothers Karamazov — Name Glossary]].

Spoiler-safe through **Book 2**.

## Book 1, Chapter 1
Fyodor Pavlovich Karamazov is introduced: a buffoonish, grasping landowner, twice married, father
of three sons he mostly neglected. The narrator sketches the family's scandalous reputation.

## Book 1, Chapter 2
Dmitri (Mitya), the eldest son by the first marriage, grows up passed between relatives; he
believes he is owed an inheritance from his mother and is now in open money conflict with his
father.

## Book 1, Chapter 3
The second marriage and its sons: Ivan, cold and intellectual, already published; and Alyosha,
the youngest, gentle and devout, who has entered the local monastery as a novice under the elder
Zosima.

## Book 1, Chapter 4
Alyosha's nature is described: not a mystic, but instinctively loving and trusted by everyone.
The narrator explains the institution of elders and Zosima's authority.

## Book 2, Chapter 1
The family gathers at the monastery, supposedly to settle the money dispute between Fyodor and
Dmitri before Zosima. Fyodor arrives already performing, unable to stop playing the fool.

## Book 2, Chapter 2
Fyodor's clowning escalates in Zosima's cell; he mocks, self-abases, and provokes. Zosima
counsels him, gently, to stop lying to himself — the root of his shamelessness.

## Book 2, Chapter 3
Zosima receives peasant women; a grieving mother is consoled. The scene establishes what his
holiness means to ordinary people.

## Book 2, Chapter 4
Madame Khokhlakova and her daughter Liza appear; Liza, an invalid, teases Alyosha, who is
embarrassed. Zosima blesses them.

## Book 2, Chapter 5
Back in the cell the conversation turns to Ivan's article on church courts. The argument circles:
if there is no immortality, is everything permitted? Dmitri arrives late.
